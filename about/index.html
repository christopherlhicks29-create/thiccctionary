A Note from the Editors

Thiccctionary is satire of a single, specific cultural habit: the internet's tendency to describe human bodies with words like thiccc, built different, built like a brick wall. We have applied that vocabulary, with full pseudo-academic seriousness, to literally anything but bodies, aircraft, championship fruit, freight vehicles, mid-century armchairs, brutalist apartment blocks, ¾-page cement mixers, the occasional architectural bridge abutment.

The rules are not negotiable. No bodies are catalogued here. No people are subjects of entries. Every photograph is of a thing. The brand promise is the joke, and the joke only lands if the rule holds.

If we have done our job, you will laugh briefly, click through three or four entries, and then, months from now, in a Costco parking lot, watching a Ford F-450 dually attempt a U-turn, describe what you are seeing as thiccc and not understand quite why. That is the highest compliment the editorial board is capable of recognizing.

For the foundational entry on the word itself, definition, etymology, and the editorial reasoning behind the third c, see thiccc, adj.
