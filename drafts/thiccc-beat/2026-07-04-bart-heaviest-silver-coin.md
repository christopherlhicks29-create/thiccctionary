# A Coin So Heavy It Can Only Be Wide

**THE THICCC BEAT:** *the desk reacts. The Senior Cataloguer receives a world record titled "heaviest" and prepares, with some relish, to reject it, before the arithmetic intervenes.*

By Bartholomew Whitmore, Senior Cataloguer

On the first of July, in Austin, Texas, a firm called Texas Precious Metals set the Guinness World Records title for the heaviest silver coin ever made: 2,500 troy ounces of .9999 fine silver, roughly 171 pounds of metal, about 200 pounds once seated in its case, struck in partnership with Sunshine Minting to commemorate the 250th anniversary of the Declaration of Independence. The record arrived at my desk with the word "heaviest" printed on it, and I will confess I reached for the standard rejection letter before the envelope was fully open.

Regular readers know the memo. [Ten days ago I ruled on the heaviest asphalt roller ever built](/articles/thiccc-beat-2026-06-24-bart-heaviest-asphalt-roller.html), and the finding was unambiguous: heavy is not thiccc. Weight is a claim about what a thing contains. Girth is a claim about how a thing sits in the world. The roller earned its entry on the drum and the drum alone. This office does not certify tonnage out of sentiment, and a record community that keeps mailing me the word "heaviest" is a record community that has not been reading its returned correspondence.

But a coin, and I say this with the reluctance of a man amending his own memo, is not a roller. A roller can hide its weight anywhere: ballast, frame, a dense conscience. A coin cannot. A coin is one material, one purity, one uniform density, in one flat circular shape. Silver is silver all the way through; .9999 fine is a purity so complete it is practically a notarization. Which means the only way this object gets to 2,500 troy ounces is by expanding in the two dimensions a coin is permitted: outward, and edgeward. The weight here is not a rival claim to girth. It is a sworn affidavit of it. For the first time in the history of this desk, the scale was measuring width by proxy, on, I am pleased to report, a named instrument: a Rice Lake Definity precision scale, provided by a dedicated scale company, read in front of an adjudicator. That is how you verify a number. The other patriotic record attempt this week remains, [per the Director of Editorial Operations, provisional](/articles/thiccc-beat-2026-07-03-constance-851000-fireworks-record.html). This one showed up with the paperwork finished.

The details reward the cataloguer. The face value is 250 dollars, engraved on 171 pounds of silver, an act of institutional understatement I intend to frame. The edge is reeded, meaning the mint looked at the circumference, the single most load-bearing surface in this publication's jurisdiction, and gave it finish work. The obverse carries a heraldic eagle and the Liberty Bell; the reverse, the hand of the Statue of Liberty with the torch and the dates 1776 to 2026. Objects, engraved on an object. The desk notes with approval that a nation chose to celebrate its semiquincentennial by minting the least portable coin in its history.

One grievance, and it is filed, not waived. The record publishes the weight to the ounce and declines to publish the diameter. A coin record that announces its weight and withholds its waistline is a filing with a page missing, and the undersigned has requested the figure through channels. The ruling below is effective regardless, because the mathematics does not require the mint's cooperation: at fixed density and coin proportions, the diameter is in there whether they print it or not.

**The ruling: Thiccc, on the geometry the weight proves.** The doctrine stands, heavy is still not thiccc, but for a uniform disc of one pure metal, weight is admissible as evidence of width, and 2,500 troy ounces is not evidence, it is a confession. Filed under Numismatics, a division this desk opened this morning for the purpose.

*Bartholomew Whitmore, Senior Cataloguer, who has requested the diameter and expects an answer in ounces.*
