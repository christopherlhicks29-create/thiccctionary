The Heaviest Garlic on Record Is Not, Technically, Garlic

Eli Hartwell, Staff Writer · the everyday-thiccc desk

THE THICCC BEAT: the desk reacts. The staff writer files from the fourth-floor walk-up, where the garlic lives in a mug by the stove and has never once been weighed.

By Eli Hartwell, Staff Writer

On the twenty-fifth of June, at a plant centre in Berkshire, a grower named Graham Barratt of Gloucestershire set a bulb of garlic on a scale and the scale said one point seven five kilograms. That is three pounds and thirteen point seven ounces of garlic. It is nineteen centimetres across, which is seven and a half inches, which is wider than this writer's face. A normal head of garlic, the kind currently going soft in the mug by this writer's stove, weighs about fifty grams. Barratt's weighs thirty-five of those. Guinness put the number in writing. By any arithmetic this desk owns, that is the heaviest garlic ever certified on this planet.

It is also, and this writer has read the fine print four times now, not garlic.

The record specimen is elephant garlic, Allium ampeloprasum, and here is what the seed catalogues will tell you if you make it past the name: elephant garlic is a leek. Not a cousin of a leek, not garlic that trains like a leek. A leek, botanically, wearing garlic's clothes, sold in garlic's aisle, having now claimed garlic's record. Culinary garlic is Allium sativum and it was not consulted. The heaviest garlic on record got to the top of the category the way a ringer wins a village raffle, by being from somewhere else entirely.

The desk has seen identity questions before. The Senior Cataloguer once stared down a pumpkin and asked whether a thing that heavy is still a pumpkin. But that was a philosophical doubt. This one has paperwork. The taxonomists settled it long before the scale got involved, and the scale, it must be said, was at Wargrave Nursery, the same establishment where Muggle, the far end of this desk's ruler, took both belts last October. Wargrave is two for two on certified girth. Somebody should check the water.

Now. This writer can hear the objection forming three desks over, so let us file it ourselves and save the memo: if the record is garlic and the object is a leek, the category is compromised and the ruling should wait. And that would be the end of it, except for one number Barratt gave the adjudicators almost in passing. Even among elephant garlic, its own kind, where bulk is the whole family business, this bulb runs four to five times the standard issue. Strip the alias off it. Judge it as what it is. It is still a monster in its own species, girth beyond the girth it was born to, and this desk's test has never been the name on the filing. The test is how the thing sits in the world. This thing sits like a planet with a stem.

And the stem is this writer's favourite witness. Bulbs grow underground, where no instrument of this publication can reach, so all season the only visible evidence was the stalk, and the stalk measured twenty centimetres around. Barratt said that is how he knew. He could not see the bulb, but the stem confessed on its behalf. The Senior Cataloguer ruled last week that for a uniform disc, weight is admissible as evidence of width. The everyday desk would like to enter the produce corollary: girth above ground is admissible as evidence of girth below it.

It should be said that the bulb earned its year. A family of moles moved in under the roots. Deer came at dawn for the leaves. In spring a huge rabbit, Barratt's words, got into the enclosure nightly, and this writer notes that the record's chief predator was itself described by size. The bulb outlasted all of them with no special treatment beyond a sheet of black weed membrane, which is to say it spent a year underground being large in secret while everything above it took turns trying to eat the evidence. Enough garlic, Guinness estimates, for one thousand four hundred portions of bolognese. This writer has costed her own bolognese against that figure and is not prepared to publish the ratio.

The ruling: Thiccc, under an assumed name. The girth is certified and the girth is real, in the garlic category where it enrolled and in the leek family where it was born, and this desk does not return a thing that wide over a clerical matter. Filed under Produce, with a cross-reference under Aliases, the first entry that division has ever needed.

This writer went home and measured the garlic in the mug. Fifteen centimetres around, and that is with the papery coat on, which the desk has decided counts as clothing.
